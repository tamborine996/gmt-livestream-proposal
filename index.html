Why staggered? Each stage de-risks the next. We commit the minimum, prove the system works on a real event, and only then spend on the permanent fixtures — so the Trust never pays for a setup it hasn't seen working.

How the trial will run

The trial runs at a lower-pressure weekday event (such as Dars-e-Quran or Gyarwee Shareef). To keep it simple, the camera sits on a table to begin with — permanent mounting is sorted later, once the best position is proven. Qasim leads the first setup and trains a second volunteer, with a one-page setup guide, so the stream never depends on one person.

What success looks like — the evidence the committee reviews before approving Stage 3:

  • A stable 1080p stream, with no reliance on mobile data
  • Clear speech audio taken from the masjid sound system
  • The event recorded and saved to YouTube for catch-up
  • Two people able to run it by following the setup guide
  • Camera position works respectfully, with no obstruction of the prayer space
03

Why it matters

Two things make this worth doing now: the access it opens up, and the fact that our current way of streaming has effectively stopped working.

The opportunity

Live streaming lets women and those who cannot attend in person — the elderly, the unwell, families tied up at home — share in religious events. Every stream is recorded on YouTube, so people can also watch afterwards, and classes and special events build into a library the community can return to. The quality of access should not depend on who happens to be in the room that day.

It also widens reach and lifts the masjid's standing. We currently stream to YouTube only; the same setup can reach YouTube and Facebook at the same time. A reliable, high-quality stream — alongside the refreshed website — gives GMT a best-in-class online presence that helps attract speakers, donations and wider engagement.

And it is a reusable, mosque-owned asset rather than a recurring outside cost. Over time it could reduce what we spend on an external provider (currently Naveed Sounds) for routine events, it supports fundraising nights by letting donors see where their money goes, and it takes the last-minute pressure off whoever is streaming.

The problem today

Currently, Qasim's phone is relied upon to live stream using 5G. Three problems make this unsustainable:

  • The sound input on Qasim's phone is broken — there has been only one live stream in 2025 and none so far in 2026.
  • 5G gives unstable quality, particularly as more attendees arrive towards the end of an event.
  • Qasim could be away or unavailable, creating a dependency on one individual that should be avoided.

A dedicated camera over a fixed broadband connection, with equipment kept at the masjid, resolves all three — and gives the masjid a reliable, repeatable way to stream rather than a setup that changes with whoever turns up.

Why these choices

Why this specific camera, and the reasoning behind every item — the problem it solves, why this option was chosen, and when to buy it.

Why the PTZOptics camera

This choice was made after detailed research and comparison against both cheaper and more expensive alternatives. The recommendation rests on three considerations: suitability, reliability and value for money.

1

Suitability

It does exactly what we need: 1080p streaming, 20× optical zoom, remote pan/tilt/zoom, preset positions (mimbar, wide hall, lecture), wall or ceiling mounting, and flexible connections. It can be controlled from a phone app or a handheld remote, and can automatically follow the speaker — so it needs little hands-on operation during an event, which makes it far easier to sustain week to week. We deliberately avoided paying extra for 4K — 1080p is ample for phones, tablets and laptops.

2

Reliability

PTZOptics is a recognised, award-winning brand across livestreaming, education, corporate AV and house-of-worship markets. The 5-year warranty (subject to confirming with the UK seller) gives confidence for regular use at Jumu'ah, Ramadan, classes and events — reducing the risk of an unknown-brand camera with uncertain support.

3

Value for money

It sits in the right middle ground. Cheaper cameras compromise on warranty, software and durability; broadcast-level models add cost for features a mosque stream won't use. We did compare a cheaper option (AVKANS), but recommend PTZOptics without reservation — its warranty, support and speaker-tracking make it better stewardship than a false economy that risks buying twice. This camera gives the features we actually need from a respected brand, without paying for the rest.

Risks & controls

The main risks, and how the staged approach keeps each one in check.

RiskHow it's controlled
Equipment is lost or stolen Everything is kept at the masjid in a lockable AV cabinet, with key access limited to trusted volunteers. Until that secure storage is in place, Qasim takes the kit home after each use.
The trial doesn't work technically The trial runs at a lower-pressure event; Stage 3 (the permanent setup) is approved only once the committee has seen the evidence.
Audio isn't clean enough Audio is taken from the masjid sound system and confirmed during the trial, before any permanent spend.
Camera position is wrong The camera is tested on a table first; nothing is drilled or mounted until the position is proven.
It depends on one person A second volunteer is trained and a one-page setup guide is written during the trial.
